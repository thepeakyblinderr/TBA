Foundation

What is a Technical Bid Analysis?

The TBA is the formal document through which an evaluator certifies whether each bidder meets the Pre-Qualification Criteria (PQC) set out in the NIT — before their price bid is even opened.

Core Purpose

IOCL conducts a Two-Bid System for most service contracts (NIT Cl. 2b). In Bid Part-I (Techno-Commercial), the evaluator checks eligibility. Only bidders who clear the TBA have their Price Bid (Part-II/BOQ) opened. This protects IOCL from awarding technically unqualified contractors on the basis of low price alone.

Cl. 2b
Type of Bid: Under Two Bid System. Techno-commercial bid is evaluated first; priced bid opening date will be available on tendering portal only after techno-commercial clearance.
Cl. 24.i
Provisions/Conditions stipulated in NIT supersede all sections of the Tender document. Therefore the TBA evaluator must always apply the NIT, not any general impression.
💡 Key Principle

The TBA format (Excel sheet) is the structured tool to document and justify each decision. Every column exists because a NIT clause demands that data point. Nothing in the format is bureaucratic — it is legally mandated evidence.

Why fill criteria BEFORE bidder names

This sequence is intentional and important. Recording the PQC benchmark first creates an objective standard that was pre-determined — not one that was adjusted after seeing what bidders submitted. This protects the integrity of the evaluation and is critical if the TBA is later scrutinized by vigilance, auditors, or a court.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Evaluators sometimes record only one slab (e.g., "1 job ≥ ₹X Lacs") and overlook that a bidder who submits 2 or 3 works of smaller individual value may still qualify under Option 2 or Option 3. Always check all three SWO options. A bidder qualifies if they satisfy any one of the three options.

Step C · TBA Columns: WO No. & Date, Job Description, Owner/End User, WO Value

Recording Work Order Details

For each bidder, the evaluator must capture every work order they have submitted — whether qualifying or not. The approach differs depending on technical acceptance or rejection.

Why capture all WOs — even non-qualifying ones for rejected bidders

TBA General Note 2 states: "In case of technical rejection, details of all submitted work orders to be given along with reason for rejection." This ensures the rejection is defensible — showing that the evaluator reviewed everything submitted, not just the convenient ones.

How to fill Work Order Details
  1. Write the WO number and issue date exactly as on the document — do not paraphrase.
  2. Summarise the Job Description concisely — focus on the nature of work (shutdown, maintenance type, equipment type) and the client's plant/industry. This is what gets compared to the Similar Nature definition.
  3. Record the Owner / End User — for sub-contract works, this is critical. The owner must have been informed about the sub-contracting (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3)).
  4. Enter the WO Value (incl. GST) from the face of the Work Order. Note: this is NOT the value used for PQC evaluation — see Step F for Executed Value. But it is recorded for reference and completeness.
Cl. 24.iii B(b)(1)
For fulfilling experience criteria, valid proof includes: Purchase/Work Order copy with Schedule of Rates/Scope of Works AND Completion Certificate by end client.
⚠️ Watch Out
  • If a WO was issued more than 7 years ago but the completion date falls within the last 7 years, it IS eligible (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(a): "irrespective of work order issue date").
  • WO value in foreign currency must be converted to INR at SBI bill selling rate on the date of WO issue (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(n)).
  • A WO executed by a bidder for their own plant/projects is NOT counted as experience (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(l)).
Step D · TBA Column: "Meeting Similar Nature of Job"

The Similar Nature Check — The Heart of the TBA

This single column — Yes or No — is arguably the most important judgment call in the entire evaluation. It requires the evaluator to compare the bidder's submitted WO against the NIT's defined scope. The definition changes with every tender — your judgment must be anchored to the specific NIT in front of you.

Cl. 11.3
NIT Cl. 11.3 defines similar nature of work specific to each tender. Always read Cl. 11.3 of the NIT you are evaluating verbatim before assessing any bidder's WO. The definition typically specifies: the type of activity (e.g., shutdown, turnaround, maintenance), the type of equipment or scope (e.g., heat exchangers, vessels, piping, electrical equipment), and the industry or plant type where the work must have been performed. All three components are usually mandatory.
💡 Illustrative Example of a Similar Nature Definition

For a mechanical shutdown tender at a refinery, a typical Cl. 11.3 might read: "Turnaround or Shutdown jobs including maintenance of heat exchangers, Columns/Vessels, Furnace, hot job works including CS/SS/AS piping in any petroleum refinery / Petrochemical Plants / Hydrocarbon Fertilizers / Hydrocarbon Industry." Notice it specifies activity type + equipment type + industry. Your specific NIT will have its own definition — which may differ significantly for civil, electrical, or instrumentation tenders.

Universal 4-Point Checklist for Any "Similar Nature" Definition
  1. Activity Type: Does the bidder's WO involve the type of activity specified in Cl. 11.3 (e.g., shutdown, turnaround, annual maintenance, civil construction)? Read the WO scope — don't rely on the WO title alone.
  2. Scope / Equipment / Work Type: Does the WO scope include the specific equipment, system, or work type listed in the definition? If the definition says "including," even one qualifying element in a broader scope can satisfy this — but confirm it is substantive, not incidental.
  3. Industry / Plant Type: Is the job site the type of industry listed in Cl. 11.3? This is often the most critical filter. A job type that matches but was performed at a non-qualifying industry cannot be accepted — industry type is not interchangeable by analogy.
  4. Verifiability from Submitted Documents: Can all of the above be confirmed from the WO copy, Schedule of Rates / Scope of Works, and/or Completion Certificate? Do not infer from the bidder's company name, reputation, or verbal claims. Evidence must be in the documents.
Why is Similar Nature defined so specifically in every NIT?

The similar nature definition ensures the contractor has hands-on experience with the specific type of work, equipment, and environment being contracted — not just generic industrial experience. For high-hazard environments, this is fundamentally a safety and technical competence requirement. For specialised civil or electrical work, it ensures familiarity with plant-specific constraints. The NIT drafter tailors the definition to match the actual risk and skill profile of the job — which is why it varies from tender to tender.

⚠️ Common Errors in Similar Nature Assessment
  • Using a definition from a previous tender — Similar nature is defined freshly in each NIT. Never carry over the definition from the last tender you evaluated, even if the scope looks similar.
  • Inferring industry type from company name alone — A company named "XYZ Refinery Services" may have done work at a textile plant. Always verify the client/plant name from the WO or CC.
  • Accepting a WO because the value is high — Value does not determine similar nature. A ₹1,000 Lacs WO at a non-qualifying industry does not meet similar nature. A ₹50 Lacs WO at a qualifying plant does (subject to value thresholds separately).
  • Rejecting a WO because the bidder was a sub-contractor — Sub-contract work is acceptable for PQC per Cl. 11.2 and Cl. 24.iii.B, subject to the required sub-contract approval documentation being submitted.
💡 Evaluator Tip

When in doubt about similar nature, refer back to the WO scope of work document (Schedule of Rates/Scope). The nature of the plant is usually mentioned in the client name or project title. If still unclear, raise a query (don't reject outright on first evaluation — see Step K).

Step E · TBA Column: "Completion Certificate Submitted?"

Verifying the Completion Certificate

The Completion Certificate (CC) is the single most important supporting document for experience. Without it, the work order — however impressive — cannot be used for qualifying.

Why is CC mandatory?

The NIT requires the executed value for PQC evaluation — not the WO value. The only document that certifies what was actually executed (and at what value) is the Completion Certificate from the end-client or EIC. It also confirms that the job was completed within the qualifying period. Without the CC, the WO is an intention — not evidence of completion.

Cl. 24.iii B(b)(2)
Completion certificate by end client with executed value, date of completion of the job and reference work order number — is required as valid proof of experience.
What must a valid Completion Certificate contain?
  1. Executed Value — the actual amount paid/certified for the work, inclusive of GST. If not explicitly stated, treat as per Cl. 24.iii.B(j) — see Step F.
  2. Date of Completion — must fall within the qualifying period (last 7 years from original bid submission date).
  3. Reference Work Order Number — to link CC to the WO submitted.
  4. Issued by end-client / EIC — not by the bidder themselves, or an affiliated entity.
⚠️ Sub-Contract Work — Additional Requirements
  • If WO is from main contractor (not end-client), the bidder must ALSO submit: Certificate from the end-user/owner confirming they were informed about or permitted the sub-contracting (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3)).
  • CC can also be from end-user / owner / main contractor / owner's consultant acting as EIC (Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(4)).
  • If CC value is ambiguous about GST inclusion, it is treated as inclusive of GST after seeking clarification (Cl. 24.iii.B(j)).
Cl. 24.iii B(k)
The CC shall separately indicate the Service Tax / GST amount included in the value, OR a separate certificate from the client mentioning the tax amount shall be submitted.
Step F · TBA Column: "Executed Value (₹ Lacs, incl. GST)"

The Executed Value — The Evaluation Currency

This is the column that decides whether a bidder meets the PQC or not. Understanding exactly what goes here — and what doesn't — is essential.

Why Executed Value — not WO Value?

Consider this: a WO may be for ₹600 Lacs but only ₹310 Lacs worth of work may have actually been completed and certified — falling short of the PQC threshold despite the impressive WO value. Conversely, a WO may be for ₹300 Lacs but scope additions and Variation Orders could mean ₹520 Lacs was actually executed — qualifying the bidder even though the WO face value appeared insufficient. (These figures are illustrative only — always use the actual values from the specific NIT.) The principle is universal: only the executed, certified value counts. This reflects actual demonstrated capability, not contractual intent.

Cl. 11.2 Note 3 & TBA Note 3
The Executed Value of completed work submitted by the bidder shall be considered during evaluation for experience criteria of PQC.
TBA Note 4
Only the executed value matching the Similar Nature of job shall be entered. If a WO covers both similar and non-similar scope, only the similar-nature portion's executed value should be considered.
How to enter Executed Value correctly
  1. Read the executed value from the Completion Certificate — not from the WO or invoice.
  2. Confirm the value is inclusive of GST. If the CC says "ex-GST" or is silent, ask for clarification per Cl. 24.iii.B(j). Until clarified, treat the stated amount as inclusive of GST.
  3. If the WO is composite (similar + non-similar scope), enter only the value attributable to the similar-nature portion. Ask the bidder for a breakdown if not clearly segregated.
  4. Compare the entered executed value against the applicable PQC slab and record whether it qualifies.
PQC OptionNo. of WorksRequired Exec. Value Each (₹ Lacs, incl. GST)How to Check
Option A1 work≥ X (as per NIT)Single qualifying WO executed value ≥ X (as per NIT)
Option B2 works≥ Y each (as per NIT)Both qualifying WOs each ≥ Y (2-job slab from NIT)

⚠️ Critical Pitfall

Do NOT add up values across WOs to meet a single slab. If a bidder claims the 1-job option, ONE single WO's executed value must independently meet that slab. You cannot combine two WOs of ₹X and ₹Y to reach the 1-job slab value. Each option is evaluated independently and the bidder must satisfy all conditions of that option with the WOs submitted under it.

Step G · TBA Column: "Annualised Value"

Annualised Value — When and Why

The annualised value column exists for ARC/AMC type tenders. Understanding when to use it (and when not to) is important for this specific tender.

What is Annualisation and why does it exist?

An Annual Rate Contract (ARC) runs for 1–3 years. If a bidder's past ARC experience ran for 3 years and generated ₹900 Lacs total, that's ₹300 Lacs per year. Comparing the total ₹900 Lacs against a benchmark set for a 1-year ARC would be unfair to bidders with shorter ARCs. Annualisation normalises all values to a per-year basis for fair comparison.

Cl. 21
Whether the tendered job is ARC/AMC: One-Time. [If ARC/AMC job, work experience value submitted by the bidder shall be annualised.]
TBA Note 6
If nature of tendered job is an ARC/AMC as per NIT, Annualized Executed Value is to be considered for evaluation.
💡 For This Tender ([Tender No.])

Since this is a One-Time (Non-ARC) job, annualisation is NOT required for the tendered job. However, if a bidder submits an ARC-type past work order as their experience, the evaluator should annualise that past WO's executed value before comparing to the PQC slab. The non-ARC experience submitted by a bidder for jobs more than one year duration also needs to be annualised for ARC-type tenders — but not for this One-Time tender.

How to calculate Annualised Value (for ARC past WOs, if applicable)
  1. Find the total executed value (from CC) and the actual duration of the contract in months.
  2. Formula: Annualised Value = (Executed Value ÷ Actual Duration in months) × 12
  3. Enter this annualised figure in the "Annualised Value" column.
  4. For this One-Time tender: enter the executed value directly, or mark N/A in the annualised column.
Step H · TBA Column: "Sub-Contracting Approval / Intimation Submitted?"

Sub-Contracting Documentation Check

When a bidder submits experience from work done as a sub-contractor (not as the main contractor for the end-client), additional documentation is mandatory.

Why is sub-contract documentation a separate check?

IOCL needs assurance that the bidder's past sub-contract work was legitimate — i.e., the end-client was aware of and sanctioned the sub-contracting. Without this, a bidder could claim experience from work they participated in informally, without proper engagement. The sub-contract approval/intimation certificate bridges the gap between the main contractor's WO and the end-client's knowledge of the actual executing party.

Cl. 24.iii B(b)(3)
In case of sub-contract works, the bidder must submit a certificate from the end user / Owner / Consultant stating that the main contractor has intimated them about the engagement of sub-contracting OR that the bidder was allowed/permitted as a sub-contractor.
Cl. 23.i
Sub-Contracting: Not permitted in this job. Note: this is about sub-contracting within the CURRENT tender — not about past experience as a sub-contractor, which is still accepted for PQC.
⚠️ Don't Confuse These Two
  • Past sub-contract experience: Acceptable for PQC, but requires approval/intimation certificate (Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3) & (4)).
  • Sub-contracting in current tender: Absolutely NOT permitted (Cl. 23.i). Any bid indicating a sub-contracting arrangement for the awarded work is liable for rejection.
Filling this column
  1. If the bidder's WO is directly from the end-client: mark "N/A" — no sub-contract approval needed.
  2. If WO is from a main contractor (not end-client): mark "Yes" if approval/intimation certificate is submitted, "No" if missing. A "No" here means the sub-contract WO cannot be used for PQC — raise a query or reject.
  3. For JV/Consortium work: also check the JV agreement and that the work order/CC is in the name of the JV or Leader with bidder's details mentioned (Cl. 24.iii.B(d)).
Step I · TBA Column: "Deviations"

Checking for Deviations

Deviations are conditions or modifications a bidder has put in their techno-commercial bid that differ from what the NIT/GCC/SCC specifies.

Why deviations must be checked in TBA

IOCL needs to know upfront if a bidder is willing to accept the full contractual terms. A deviation on a critical commercial or safety clause discovered after award creates disputes and potential losses. The TBA flags deviations so they can be resolved — or the bid rejected — before the price bid is opened.

Cl. 23.e
No deviation to GCC/SCC is permitted in this tendered job.
Cl. 24.v B(c)
Bid is liable for rejection if the bidder stipulates his own conditions and refuses to withdraw them.
How to handle deviations
  1. Review the bidder's techno-commercial bid for any conditions, caveats, or modifications to GCC/SCC terms.
  2. If deviation found: record it specifically in the Deviations column (e.g., "Bidder has sought deviation on defect liability period").
  3. Since no deviation is permitted (Cl. 23.e), raise a query asking the bidder to unconditionally withdraw the deviation.
  4. If the bidder refuses to withdraw in query reply: recommend rejection citing Cl. 24.v.B(c).
  5. If no deviations: write "NIL" — never leave blank, as blank implies the column was not checked.
Step J · TBA Column: "Whether meeting Addl. Tech. PQC"

Additional Technical PQC Check

Some tenders have additional technical requirements beyond the standard ATO and SWO criteria — for example, specific certifications, equipment ownership, or key personnel qualifications.

Cl. 12
Additional Technical Requirement (PQC), if any: Not Applicable
💡 For This Tender

Always check Cl. 12 of the specific NIT being evaluated. If it says "Not Applicable," mark this column as "N/A" for all bidders — never leave blank. In tenders where Addl. PQC is applicable (e.g., specific equipment ownership, key personnel qualifications, ISO certifications), this column becomes a pass/fail gate and must be evaluated rigorously for each bidder.

Why this column exists even when N/A

The TBA format is a universal document used across all IOCL tenders. By always having this column, evaluators are forced to actively check the NIT for any additional requirements rather than assuming there are none. The N/A entry is an affirmative confirmation, not an omission.

Step K · TBA Column: "Remarks"

Remarks — The Most Important Column to Write Well

The Remarks column is where the evaluator's professional judgment is documented. A vague remark is almost as bad as no remark. Here's how to write remarks that stand up to scrutiny.

Why Remarks must be specific and clause-referenced

The TBA is a legal document. If a rejected bidder challenges the decision — through court, arbitration, or CVC inquiry — the Remarks column is the evaluator's primary defence. Generic remarks like "does not meet PQC" are insufficient. Specific remarks like "Executed value of ₹[___] Lacs (from CC dated [date]) is below the required ₹[Z] Lacs for 3-work option as per NIT Cl. 11.2" are defensible.

How to write good Remarks
  1. For Accepted bidders: Note the qualifying WO(s) and the specific PQC option met (e.g., "Qualifies under 2-job option: WO#1 executed value ₹[___]L and WO#2 executed value ₹[___]L, both ≥ ₹[Y]L as per Cl. 11.2 Option 2").
  2. For Queries: State specifically what is missing or unclear (e.g., "CC for WO#2 does not mention GST component. Bidder asked to submit revised CC or separate certificate per Cl. 24.iii.B(k)").
  3. For Rejected bidders: State the specific deficiency with values and clause reference (e.g., "Highest executed value of submitted similar WO is ₹[___]L, below ₹[X]L required for 1-job option, and only 1 similar WO submitted — Option B and C not applicable. Offer rejected per Cl. 24.v.A(a)").
Cl. 24.vii
Offers with incomplete/irrelevant documents or anomalies are liable for rejection without any communication. Hence bidders are advised to take utmost care while uploading prequalification documents.
💡 Query vs. Rejection

Not every deficiency warrants immediate rejection. On Initial TBA, raise a query for: missing documents that could be submitted, unclear values that could be clarified, or ambiguous scope descriptions. Reserve outright rejection for: clear non-fulfillment of PQC values, forged/misrepresented documents, or refusal to withdraw unacceptable conditions.

The last column crystallises all preceding evaluation into a single decision: Accepted, Rejected, or Query. This drives everything that happens next.

⚠️ Signature & Approval

The completed TBA must be signed by the evaluator (Prepared By), reviewed by a senior officer (Checked By), and approved by DGM (Contracts) as per IWPM Clause 4.3.1 + R&R Cl. 20(viii). An unsigned TBA has no formal validity and cannot be used as basis for bid opening or rejection communication.

Evaluator's Toolkit

Standard Queries to Raise When Documents Are Unclear

On Initial TBA, when a document is ambiguous or incomplete — but the deficiency could potentially be resolved — issue a formal query rather than outright rejecting. These are the standard query situations for this tender, with the exact clause basis and suggested wording.

The Purpose of a Query

A query is not a favour to the bidder — it is a procedural safeguard for the evaluator. By issuing a query, the evaluator demonstrates due diligence: that no bidder was rejected on a technicality that could have been clarified. However, a query cannot be used to allow a bidder to submit new experience, change values, or cure a substantive PQC shortfall. Queries are for clarification of what was already submitted, not for fresh submissions.

💡 Golden Rule on Queries

Query = clarify existing information. You cannot query a bidder to ask them to submit a new WO, increase a value, or add experience they did not already claim. Queries are only valid for: missing supporting documents for already-claimed experience, ambiguous values in submitted documents, and unclear descriptions that need elaboration.

Category 1 — Completion Certificate Issues
Query Q-1A
When to raise: CC submitted but does not separately state the GST amount included in the executed value, making it unclear whether the value is inclusive or exclusive of GST.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(k) — CC shall separately indicate the Service Tax/GST amount, OR a separate certificate from the client shall be submitted.

Suggested query wording:
"The Completion Certificate submitted for WO No. [___] dated [___] mentions an executed value of ₹[___]. It is not clear whether this value is inclusive or exclusive of GST/Service Tax. As required under NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(k), please submit either (a) a revised CC clearly mentioning the GST component, or (b) a separate certificate from the end-client confirming the GST amount included in the executed value."
Query Q-1B
When to raise: CC submitted does not mention the completion date, making it impossible to verify whether the job falls within the 7-year qualifying period.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2) — CC must state date of completion of the job.

Suggested query wording:
"The Completion Certificate for WO No. [___] does not mention the date of completion of the work. As per NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2), the Completion Certificate must state the date of completion. Please submit a revised or supplementary certificate from the end-client/EIC confirming the date of completion of the referenced job."
Query Q-1C
When to raise: CC is issued by the main contractor (not the end-client/EIC) and no certificate from the end-user/owner is submitted to confirm knowledge of sub-contracting.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3) — for sub-contract works, a certificate from end-user/owner/consultant confirming they were informed about or permitted the sub-contracting is required.

Suggested query wording:
"WO No. [___] appears to be a sub-contract WO issued by [main contractor name] as the main contractor. As per NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3), for sub-contract experience, the bidder must submit a certificate from the end-user/owner/consultant confirming that the main contractor had intimated them about the engagement of a sub-contractor, OR that the bidder was permitted as a sub-contractor. Please submit such a certificate for the above WO."
Query Q-1D
When to raise: No CC submitted at all for a claimed WO, but the WO itself and a payment certificate / running account bill are submitted instead.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(1) and B(b)(2) — both WO copy AND Completion Certificate are separately required as mandatory proof.

Suggested query wording:
"For WO No. [___], a Completion Certificate from the end-client/EIC as required under NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2) has not been submitted. The payment certificate/RA bill submitted is not a substitute for the Completion Certificate. Please submit a Completion Certificate issued by the end-client/owner/consultant/EIC, mentioning the executed value and date of completion, as per Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2)."
⚖
⚖ Common Evaluator Debate — Read This Carefully
"The WO value is only ₹10 Lacs — there is no point asking for the CC since the executed value can never meet the SWO threshold anyway."
THIS REASONING IS PROCEDURALLY INCORRECT

This is one of the most common shortcuts taken by evaluators — and it is wrong for the following reasons:

1. Both documents are independently mandatory. NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(1) requires the WO copy. Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2) requires the Completion Certificate. These are not conditional on each other. The NIT does not say "submit CC only if WO value looks promising." The clause is unconditional — both are required as valid proof of experience.

2. WO value ≠ Executed value. A ₹10L WO could have been amended upward through Variation Orders or scope additions. You cannot assume the executed value equals the WO face value. The only document that certifies what was actually paid/completed is the CC. Pre-judging based on WO value is an assumption — not an evaluation.

3. The TBA executed value column cannot be filled from a WO alone. If you don't have a CC, the "Executed Value" column in the TBA has no data to populate. You cannot enter the WO value as a proxy — that is factually incorrect and will not survive scrutiny.

4. Skipping the query creates an uneven playing field. If you raise queries for some missing CCs but not others (even if the "others" are small WOs), you are applying procedure selectively. A rejected bidder can challenge this as inconsistent evaluation. Uniform procedure protects the evaluator.

5. TBA General Note 2 requires all submitted WOs to be documented. "In case of technical rejection, details of all submitted work orders to be given along with reason for rejection." If you silently ignore a ₹10L WO without even querying for its CC, you have no documented basis for excluding it. The correct basis — to record in Remarks — is: "CC not submitted for WO No. [___]. Query raised. No response received / CC not submitted even after query. Executed value cannot be verified per Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2). WO not considered."

✓ The Correct Approach

Always raise Query Q-1D for every WO that has no CC — regardless of the WO value. If the bidder submits the CC and the executed value is ₹8L, record it in the TBA and mark the WO as not meeting PQC threshold. If the bidder doesn't respond, reject that specific WO for missing mandatory document. Either way, your TBA is airtight and your procedure is uniform. The extra query takes 2 minutes. The procedural protection it gives you is invaluable.

Category 2 — Similar Nature Ambiguity
Query Q-2A
When to raise: WO/CC scope description is vague — mentions "maintenance job" or "shutdown work" but does not clearly state the industry type (petroleum refinery, petrochemical, etc.).

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 11.3 — similar nature requires the job to be in a qualifying industry; evidence must be verifiable from submitted documents.

Suggested query wording:
"The scope of work mentioned in WO No. [___] / CC refers to [shutdown / maintenance / turnaround] work but does not clearly specify the nature of the plant/facility where the work was carried out. As per NIT Cl. 11.3, similar nature experience must be in a petroleum refinery / petrochemical plant / hydrocarbon fertilizer / hydrocarbon industry. Please submit supporting documents (e.g., client profile, plant description, scope elaboration) to establish that the job site qualifies as one of the above industry types."
Query Q-2B
When to raise: WO covers a composite scope (both similar and non-similar work) and the CC gives a single executed value without bifurcating the similar-nature portion.

Clause basis: TBA General Note 4 — only the executed value matching the similar nature of job shall be entered.

Suggested query wording:
"WO No. [___] appears to include scope beyond the similar nature defined in NIT Cl. 11.3 (e.g., civil works / non-shutdown maintenance). The Completion Certificate provides a composite executed value of ₹[___] without bifurcation. Please submit a certificate from the end-client/EIC separately confirming the executed value attributable specifically to the shutdown / turnaround / qualifying scope of work, for consideration under NIT Cl. 11.2 and TBA Note 4."
Query Q-2C
When to raise: Equipment type is not mentioned — the CC describes "shutdown job at refinery" but doesn't specify whether it involved heat exchangers, columns/vessels, furnace, or piping as required by Cl. 11.3.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 11.3 — similar nature must include maintenance of heat exchangers, columns/vessels, furnace, or hot job works with CS/SS/AS piping.

Suggested query wording:
"The submitted WO/CC for WO No. [___] describes a shutdown job at [plant/refinery name] but does not specify the type of equipment maintained. As per NIT Cl. 11.3, the qualifying scope must include maintenance of heat exchangers, columns/vessels, furnace, or hot job works with CS/SS/AS piping. Please submit the Schedule of Rates / Scope of Work document or a clarification letter from the client specifying the equipment/activities carried out during the shutdown."
Category 3 — Work Order Date & Period Issues
Query Q-3A
When to raise: WO was issued before the 7-year qualifying window, but it is not clear from the CC whether the job was completed within the qualifying period.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(a) — WO issued before qualifying period is acceptable IF the job was completed/in progress during the qualifying period (irrespective of WO issue date).

Suggested query wording:
"WO No. [___] was issued on [date], which is outside the 7-year qualifying period from [qualifying period start]. However, as per NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(a), WOs issued prior to the qualifying period may still be considered if the job was completed or was in progress during the qualifying period. The submitted CC does not clearly state the date of completion. Please submit a Completion Certificate or documentary evidence confirming that work under WO No. [___] was completed or in progress on or after [qualifying period start date]."
Query Q-3B
When to raise: WO value is stated in a foreign currency and the bidder has not provided the conversion rate or the converted INR value.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(n) — foreign currency WO values to be converted to INR at SBI bill selling rate on the date of WO issue.

Suggested query wording:
"WO No. [___] states the contract value in [currency name]. As per NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(n), the value must be converted to INR at the SBI bill selling rate on the date of WO issue (i.e., [WO issue date]). Please submit: (a) the SBI bill selling rate for [currency] on [WO date] with supporting evidence (e.g., RBI/SBI exchange rate statement), and (b) the resulting converted INR value for the executed amount as per the CC."
Query Q-3C
When to raise: A WO submitted for PQC experience shows the job as under execution / running at the time of bid submission — the bidder has not submitted a Completion Certificate because the contract period stated in the WO had not expired. However, the WO was issued some time ago and it is possible the job has since been completed; the bidder may simply have forgotten to submit the updated CC.

Why this matters: A running WO without a CC cannot be evaluated — there is no executed value to compare against the PQC threshold. However, outright exclusion without a query would be unfair if the job was in fact completed. The evaluator's duty is to seek clarification, not to assume either way.

Clause basis: NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2) — CC required with executed value and date of completion. NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(a) — qualifying period reckoned from completion date.

Suggested query wording:
"WO No. [___] dated [___] submitted in support of PQC experience appears to be under execution / the contractual period had not expired as of the bid submission date, and no Completion Certificate has been submitted. If this work has since been completed, please submit a Completion Certificate from the end-client/EIC confirming: (a) the date of completion, (b) the executed value inclusive of GST, and (c) reference to the above WO. If the work is still ongoing and a CC cannot be submitted, please confirm the same in writing — the WO will not be considered for PQC evaluation as executed value cannot be verified per NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(2)."
💡 Key Concept — Running WO: Query First, Decide After

A WO marked as "under execution" is not automatically disqualified — it is a queryable deficiency. Consider what is actually possible:

  • Job completed, bidder forgot to attach CC — The most common scenario. A query gives them one fair opportunity to submit the CC. After receiving it, evaluate normally on executed value and completion date.
  • Job genuinely still running — If the bidder confirms this in response to the query, the WO cannot be considered. No executed value exists and the work is not "completed" per Cl. 11.2.
  • CC received but completion date outside qualifying period — WO is ineligible. Exclude with clause reference recorded in Remarks.
  • CC received and completion date within qualifying period — Evaluate on executed value from CC exactly as any other WO.

Never silently exclude a running WO without raising a query — doing so deprives the bidder of a fair opportunity and creates a procedural vulnerability if the decision is later challenged.

Category 5 — NIT-Specific Special Provisions (Alternate Document Routes)
What are special provisions and why do they exist?

Not every IOCL tender is identical. Some NITs include additional sub-clauses under Cl. 24.iii.B(b) that create alternate document routes for specific types of work where a standard Completion Certificate may not be the norm — for example, supply-only contracts, equipment installation jobs, or highly specialised scopes where the industry practice is to certify delivery rather than completion. These provisions recognise that the strict CC requirement, while appropriate for most service contracts, can be impractical for certain niche WO types. The key principle: the alternate route still requires proof of execution — it only changes the form that proof takes.

📋 The Evaluator's First Step — Always Read the Entire NIT

Before beginning any TBA evaluation, read Cl. 24.iii.B(b) in full in the specific NIT you are working on. Do not assume the document requirements are identical to the last tender you evaluated. Special provisions are tender-specific — they appear only when the NIT drafter has included them for a reason relevant to that particular scope of work.

Ask yourself three questions as you read:

  1. Does this NIT have any sub-clauses beyond the standard (1), (2), (3), (4)?
  2. If yes — what specific WO/work type does each special provision apply to?
  3. Has the bidder submitted a WO that could fall under one of these special provisions?
💡 How Special Provisions Typically Work

A special provision usually has the following structure in the NIT: "In case the bidder has submitted WO/PO pertaining to [specific work type], any one of the following documents may be considered as valid proof…" followed by a list of alternate documents. The pattern is consistent even if the specific work type and documents vary. Once you recognise this pattern, you can apply the same evaluation logic regardless of which special provision you encounter.

Universal evaluation approach for any special provision
  1. Confirm the provision exists in this NIT. Never apply a special provision from memory or from a previous tender. Find it in the current NIT and read it verbatim. If it is not there, the standard CC route applies without exception.
  2. Confirm the WO actually falls within the scope of the provision. Special provisions are narrow — they apply to a defined WO type only. Read the WO/PO description and verify it matches the specific scope described in the provision. When in doubt, treat it as a standard WO requiring a CC.
  3. Check what the provision permits as alternate proof. Identify the list of acceptable alternate documents. Note whether any one document suffices or whether a combination is required (typically: the WO/PO copy PLUS one alternate proof of execution).
  4. Verify the alternate document actually proves execution. The purpose of a CC is to confirm the work was done and certify the value. Whatever alternate document is submitted must serve the same purpose — it must establish what was executed and at what value. A document that only proves the order was placed (not executed) is not sufficient under any route.
  5. Check if a catch-all "any other document" option exists. Many such provisions include a final option allowing "any other document" submitted by the bidder — subject to IOCL's scrutiny. This is not a blank cheque. It requires active evaluation and, given its discretionary nature, should be escalated to your checking officer before acceptance. Document your reasoning in Remarks.
  6. Record the specific provision applied in the TBA Remarks. Do not just write "documents verified." State which sub-clause was applied, which document was accepted, and what value was established. This creates a clear audit trail.
Query Q-5A
When to raise: A special provision exists in the NIT for the type of WO submitted by the bidder, but the bidder has only submitted the WO/PO copy — without any of the alternate execution proof documents listed in the provision.

Clause basis: The relevant special sub-clause of Cl. 24.iii.B(b) in the current NIT — the WO/PO copy alone is never sufficient proof of execution under any route.

Suggested query wording:
"WO/PO No. [___] dated [___] pertaining to [describe work type as per NIT special provision] has been submitted. As per NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)[sub-clause no.], in addition to the WO/PO copy, proof of execution in the form of [list the alternate documents permitted by the provision] must also be submitted. No such accompanying document establishing execution has been found in your submission. Please submit the appropriate document(s) as listed in NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)[sub-clause no.] at the earliest."
Query Q-5B
When to raise: The bidder has submitted a "catch-all" or "any other document" as their alternate proof of execution, but it is vague, undated, or does not clearly establish the executed value or the nature/scope of the work.

Clause basis: The catch-all option in most special provisions is explicitly conditional on being "properly checked by IOCL" — giving the evaluator the authority and obligation to seek clarification.

Suggested query wording:
"In support of WO/PO No. [___], you have submitted [document name/type] as alternate proof of execution. However, this document does not clearly establish: (a) the value of work actually executed/delivered, and/or (b) confirmation that the scope described in the WO/PO was completed. Please submit a supplementary document from the client or a more specific document that confirms the executed value and the nature/scope of work completed, for IOCL's proper verification as required under NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)[sub-clause no.]."
⚠️ Critical Mistakes to Avoid with Special Provisions
  • Applying a provision from a different tender — Special provisions are NIT-specific. Just because a provision existed in the last tender you evaluated does not mean it exists here. Always verify in the current NIT before applying.
  • Applying a provision to a WO type it was not designed for — Read the scope described in the provision carefully. If the bidder's WO is different from what the provision covers, the standard CC route applies. Do not stretch the provision's applicability.
  • Accepting WO/PO alone as sufficient proof — Under every route — standard or special — a WO/PO copy establishes only that an order was placed. Proof of execution is always required in addition. No provision removes this requirement; it only changes the form the proof must take.
  • Rubber-stamping the "any other document" option — When a provision allows "any other document," it is invariably conditioned on proper checking by IOCL. This is an active duty of scrutiny, not a passive acceptance. If you cannot clearly establish executed value and scope from the document, raise Query Q-5B and escalate to your checking officer.
  • Not recording the specific provision applied in Remarks — If you accept a WO under a special provision without recording which sub-clause and which alternate document was relied upon, there is no audit trail. Any subsequent scrutiny — audit, vigilance, court — will question how the acceptance was justified.
🚫 What CANNOT Be Queried — Must Lead to Rejection
  • Executed value is simply below PQC threshold — If the bidder's best similar-nature executed value is below the minimum SWO slab under all three options, this is a substantive shortfall. No query can fix it; reject with clause reference.
  • No similar-nature WO submitted at all — Cannot query a bidder to submit new experience WOs they did not include in their original bid.
  • Job site is clearly a non-qualifying industry — If the WO is from a steel plant or cement factory, no clarification can change the industry type; reject under Cl. 11.3.
  • Sub-contracting arrangement for current job — If a bidder indicates they will sub-contract work under this tender, this violates Cl. 23.i and is grounds for immediate rejection; cannot be cured by query.
  • Deviation to GCC/SCC submitted — NIT Cl. 23.e explicitly states deviations are not permitted. A bidder cannot withdraw their deviation through a query; the offer is non-compliant.
  • EMD not submitted at all — Missing EMD (unless bidder is exempt under Cl. 10 as MSE/DPIIT/PSU) is not a queryable deficiency; the bid is liable for rejection.

💡 Key Concept — Reading "Repair and Maintenance of Civil Works"

The Cl. 11.3 definition has two limbs separated by a slash: "Repair and maintenance of civil works / Civil construction works." The slash means either limb qualifies independently.

Within the first limb, "repair and maintenance" is a compound phrase describing a single recognised category of civil work — it does not mean a WO must contain both repair activities and maintenance activities simultaneously. In practice:

  • Repair works only (e.g., WO for repair of roads and drains) → Qualifies — repair is within the "repair and maintenance" category.
  • Maintenance works only (e.g., WO for annual maintenance of civil infrastructure) → Qualifies — maintenance is within the "repair and maintenance" category.
  • Both repair and maintenance together → Also qualifies.

Conceptual distinction: Repair is corrective — restoring something that has deteriorated or failed (e.g., patching a broken road, fixing a cracked drain). Maintenance is preventive — upkeep to prevent deterioration (e.g., repainting, clearing drainage, periodic inspection and minor works). Both fall under the same category of civil work in any industrial facility, and IOCL's definition groups them together deliberately: contractors who do one routinely do the other. A WO describing either activity at a qualifying industry is sufficient — do not reject a WO simply because it says "repair" without using the word "maintenance," or vice versa.

What to watch for: The WO scope must clearly indicate civil works — e.g., roads, pavements, drains, structures, buildings, RCC works. A WO described only as "housekeeping," "cleaning," or "painting" without reference to civil works does not qualify even if executed at a petroleum refinery.

⚠️ Key Learning — Sub-contracting: Experience vs Current Bid

There are two distinct sub-contracting questions in every TBA: (1) Does the bidder's experience come from sub-contract work? (This is about their PQC documents — and is acceptable per Cl. 11.2, subject to end-user certificate.) (2) Does the bidder propose to sub-contract the current job? (This is about compliance with NIT Cl. 23.j — and when prohibited, it makes the bid non-compliant regardless of experience quality.) These are completely independent questions with different consequences. Confusing the two is a common evaluator error.
