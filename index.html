Foundation

What is a Technical Bid Analysis?

The TBA is the formal document through which an evaluator certifies whether each bidder meets the Pre-Qualification Criteria (PQC) set out in the NIT — before their price bid is even opened.

Core Purpose

IOCL conducts a Two-Bid System (NIT Cl. 2b). In Bid Part-I (Techno-Commercial), the evaluator checks eligibility. Only bidders who clear the TBA have their Price Bid (Part-II/BOQ) opened. This protects IOCL from awarding technically unqualified contractors on the basis of low price alone.

Cl. 2b
Type of Bid: Under Two Bid System. Techno-commercial bid is evaluated first; priced bid opening date will be available on tendering portal only after techno-commercial clearance.
Cl. 24.i
Provisions/Conditions stipulated in NIT supersede all sections of the Tender document. Therefore the TBA evaluator must always apply the NIT, not any general impression.
💡 Key Principle

The TBA format (Excel sheet) is the structured tool to document and justify each decision. Every column exists because a NIT clause demands that data point. Nothing in the format is bureaucratic — it is legally mandated evidence.

Why fill criteria BEFORE bidder names
Why fill criteria BEFORE bidder names

This sequence is intentional and important. Recording the PQC benchmark first creates an objective standard that was pre-determined — not one that was adjusted after seeing what bidders submitted. This protects the integrity of the evaluation and is critical if the TBA is later scrutinized by vigilance, auditors, or a court.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Evaluators sometimes record only one slab (e.g., "1 job ≥ ₹547.26 Lacs") and overlook that a bidder who submits 2 or 3 works of smaller individual value may still qualify. Always check all three options. A bidder qualifies if they satisfy any one of the three options.

Step C · TBA Columns: WO No. & Date, Job Description, Owner/End User, WO Value

Recording Work Order Details

For each bidder, the evaluator must capture every work order they have submitted — whether qualifying or not. The approach differs depending on technical acceptance or rejection.

Why capture all WOs — even non-qualifying ones for rejected bidders

TBA General Note 2 states: "In case of technical rejection, details of all submitted work orders to be given along with reason for rejection." This ensures the rejection is defensible — showing that the evaluator reviewed everything submitted, not just the convenient ones.

How to fill Work Order Details
  1. Write the WO number and issue date exactly as on the document — do not paraphrase.
  2. Summarise the Job Description concisely — focus on the nature of work (shutdown, maintenance type, equipment type) and the client's plant/industry. This is what gets compared to the Similar Nature definition.
  3. Record the Owner / End User — for sub-contract works, this is critical. The owner must have been informed about the sub-contracting (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3)).
  4. Enter the WO Value (incl. GST) from the face of the Work Order. Note: this is NOT the value used for PQC evaluation — see Step F for Executed Value. But it is recorded for reference and completeness.
Cl. 24.iii B(b)(1)
For fulfilling experience criteria, valid proof includes: Purchase/Work Order copy with Schedule of Rates/Scope of Works AND Completion Certificate by end client.
⚠️ Watch Out
  • If a WO was issued more than 7 years ago but the completion date falls within the last 7 years, it IS eligible (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(a): "irrespective of work order issue date").
  • WO value in foreign currency must be converted to INR at SBI bill selling rate on the date of WO issue (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(n)).
  • A WO executed by a bidder for their own plant/projects is NOT counted as experience (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(l)).
Step D · TBA Column: "Meeting Similar Nature of Job"

The Similar Nature Check — The Heart of the TBA

This single column — Yes or No — is arguably the most important judgment call in the entire evaluation. It requires the evaluator to compare the bidder's submitted WO against the NIT's defined scope.

Cl. 11.3
Similar nature is defined as: "Turnaround or Shutdown jobs including maintenance of heat exchangers, Columns/Vessels, Furnace, hot job works including CS, SS or AS piping in any petroleum refinery / Petrochemical Plants / Hydrocarbon Fertilizers / Hydrocarbon Industry."
4-Point Checklist for "Similar Nature"
  1. Activity Type: Is the work a Turnaround (TAR) or Shutdown job? A routine maintenance contract without shutdown context does not qualify automatically.
  2. Equipment Type: Does the scope include at least one of: Heat Exchangers, Columns/Vessels, Furnace? Or hot job works with CS/SS/AS piping? The definition uses "including" — so even one of these in a broader scope can qualify.
  3. Industry: Is the job site a Petroleum Refinery, Petrochemical Plant, Hydrocarbon Fertilizer Plant, or Hydrocarbon Industry? A shutdown job at a power plant or cement plant does NOT qualify.
  4. Evidence in Documents: Can all of the above be verified from the WO description/scope and/or completion certificate? Do not infer from company name alone.
Why is Similar Nature defined so specifically?

DHDS (Diesel Hydro Desulphurization) M&I (Maintenance & Inspection) Shutdown involves high-pressure, high-temperature process equipment in a hydrogen-rich environment. The NIT similar nature definition ensures that the bidder has hands-on experience with the specific types of equipment and hazardous conditions at refineries — not just generic industrial maintenance. This is a safety and technical competence requirement.

⚠️ Common Errors in Similar Nature Assessment
  • Marking a fertilizer plant WO as "Not Similar" — Hydrocarbon Fertilizer Plants are explicitly included in Cl. 11.3.
  • Accepting a WO from a general engineering company just because the job description says "maintenance" — must confirm it was at a qualifying plant type.
  • Rejecting a WO because the bidder was a sub-contractor — sub-contract work is acceptable per Cl. 11.2 and Cl. 24.iii.B, subject to sub-contract approval documentation.
💡 Evaluator Tip

When in doubt about similar nature, refer back to the WO scope of work document (Schedule of Rates/Scope). The nature of the plant is usually mentioned in the client name or project title. If still unclear, raise a query (don't reject outright on first evaluation — see Step K).

Step E · TBA Column: "Completion Certificate Submitted?"

Verifying the Completion Certificate

The Completion Certificate (CC) is the single most important supporting document for experience. Without it, the work order — however impressive — cannot be used for qualifying.

Why is CC mandatory?

The NIT requires the executed value for PQC evaluation — not the WO value. The only document that certifies what was actually executed (and at what value) is the Completion Certificate from the end-client or EIC. It also confirms that the job was completed within the qualifying period. Without the CC, the WO is an intention — not evidence of completion.

Cl. 24.iii B(b)(2)
Completion certificate by end client with executed value, date of completion of the job and reference work order number — is required as valid proof of experience.
What must a valid Completion Certificate contain?
  1. Executed Value — the actual amount paid/certified for the work, inclusive of GST. If not explicitly stated, treat as per Cl. 24.iii.B(j) — see Step F.
  2. Date of Completion — must fall within the qualifying period (last 7 years from original bid submission date).
  3. Reference Work Order Number — to link CC to the WO submitted.
  4. Issued by end-client / EIC — not by the bidder themselves, or an affiliated entity.
⚠️ Sub-Contract Work — Additional Requirements
  • If WO is from main contractor (not end-client), the bidder must ALSO submit: Certificate from the end-user/owner confirming they were informed about or permitted the sub-contracting (NIT Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3)).
  • CC can also be from end-user / owner / main contractor / owner's consultant acting as EIC (Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(4)).
  • If CC value is ambiguous about GST inclusion, it is treated as inclusive of GST after seeking clarification (Cl. 24.iii.B(j)).
Cl. 24.iii B(k)
The CC shall separately indicate the Service Tax / GST amount included in the value, OR a separate certificate from the client mentioning the tax amount shall be submitted.
Step F · TBA Column: "Executed Value (₹ Lacs, incl. GST)"

The Executed Value — The Evaluation Currency

This is the column that decides whether a bidder meets the PQC or not. Understanding exactly what goes here — and what doesn't — is essential.

Why Executed Value — not WO Value?

A Work Order may be for ₹600 Lacs but only ₹310 Lacs worth of work may have actually been completed and certified. Conversely, a WO may be for ₹300 Lacs but scope additions could mean ₹520 Lacs was actually executed. The NIT is clear: the executed (certified) value is what counts. This reflects actual demonstrated capability, not contractual intent.

Cl. 11.2 Note 3 & TBA Note 3
The Executed Value of completed work submitted by the bidder shall be considered during evaluation for experience criteria of PQC.
TBA Note 4
Only the executed value matching the Similar Nature of job shall be entered. If a WO covers both similar and non-similar scope, only the similar-nature portion's executed value should be considered.
How to enter Executed Value correctly
  1. Read the executed value from the Completion Certificate — not from the WO or invoice.
  2. Confirm the value is inclusive of GST. If the CC says "ex-GST" or is silent, ask for clarification per Cl. 24.iii.B(j). Until clarified, treat the stated amount as inclusive of GST.
  3. If the WO is composite (similar + non-similar scope), enter only the value attributable to the similar-nature portion. Ask the bidder for a breakdown if not clearly segregated.
  4. Compare the entered executed value against the applicable PQC slab and record whether it qualifies.
PQC OptionNo. of WorksRequired Exec. Value Each (₹ Lacs, incl. GST)How to Check
Option A1 work≥ 547.26Single qualifying WO executed value ≥ 547.26
Option B2 works≥ 437.81 eachBoth qualifying WOs each ≥ 437.81
Option C3 works≥ 328.36 eachAll three qualifying WOs each ≥ 328.36
⚠️ Critical Pitfall

Do NOT add up values across WOs to meet a single slab. If a bidder claims 1-job option, ONE single WO's executed value must be ≥ ₹547.26 Lacs. You cannot combine two WOs of ₹300 Lacs each to reach ₹600 Lacs for the 1-job option. Each option is evaluated independently.

Step G · TBA Column: "Annualised Value"

Annualised Value — When and Why

The annualised value column exists for ARC/AMC type tenders. Understanding when to use it (and when not to) is important for this specific tender.

What is Annualisation and why does it exist?

An Annual Rate Contract (ARC) runs for 1–3 years. If a bidder's past ARC experience ran for 3 years and generated ₹900 Lacs total, that's ₹300 Lacs per year. Comparing the total ₹900 Lacs against a benchmark set for a 1-year ARC would be unfair to bidders with shorter ARCs. Annualisation normalises all values to a per-year basis for fair comparison.

Cl. 21
Whether the tendered job is ARC/AMC: One-Time. [If ARC/AMC job, work experience value submitted by the bidder shall be annualised.]
TBA Note 6
If nature of tendered job is an ARC/AMC as per NIT, Annualized Executed Value is to be considered for evaluation.
💡 For This Tender (9030C26A47)

Since this is a One-Time (Non-ARC) job, annualisation is NOT required for the tendered job. However, if a bidder submits an ARC-type past work order as their experience, the evaluator should annualise that past WO's executed value before comparing to the PQC slab. The non-ARC experience submitted by a bidder for jobs more than one year duration also needs to be annualised for ARC-type tenders — but not for this One-Time tender.

How to calculate Annualised Value (for ARC past WOs, if applicable)
  1. Find the total executed value (from CC) and the actual duration of the contract in months.
  2. Formula: Annualised Value = (Executed Value ÷ Actual Duration in months) × 12
  3. Enter this annualised figure in the "Annualised Value" column.
  4. For this One-Time tender: enter the executed value directly, or mark N/A in the annualised column.
Step H · TBA Column: "Sub-Contracting Approval / Intimation Submitted?"

Sub-Contracting Documentation Check

When a bidder submits experience from work done as a sub-contractor (not as the main contractor for the end-client), additional documentation is mandatory.

Why is sub-contract documentation a separate check?

IOCL needs assurance that the bidder's past sub-contract work was legitimate — i.e., the end-client was aware of and sanctioned the sub-contracting. Without this, a bidder could claim experience from work they participated in informally, without proper engagement. The sub-contract approval/intimation certificate bridges the gap between the main contractor's WO and the end-client's knowledge of the actual executing party.

Cl. 24.iii B(b)(3)
In case of sub-contract works, the bidder must submit a certificate from the end user / Owner / Consultant stating that the main contractor has intimated them about the engagement of sub-contracting OR that the bidder was allowed/permitted as a sub-contractor.
Cl. 23.i
Sub-Contracting: Not permitted in this job. Note: this is about sub-contracting within the CURRENT tender — not about past experience as a sub-contractor, which is still accepted for PQC.
⚠️ Don't Confuse These Two
  • Past sub-contract experience: Acceptable for PQC, but requires approval/intimation certificate (Cl. 24.iii.B(b)(3) & (4)).
  • Sub-contracting in current tender: Absolutely NOT permitted (Cl. 23.i). Any bid indicating a sub-contracting arrangement for the awarded work is liable for rejection.
Filling this column
  1. If the bidder's WO is directly from the end-client: mark "N/A" — no sub-contract approval needed.
  2. If WO is from a main contractor (not end-client): mark "Yes" if approval/intimation certificate is submitted, "No" if missing. A "No" here means the sub-contract WO cannot be used for PQC — raise a query or reject.
  3. For JV/Consortium work: also check the JV agreement and that the work order/CC is in the name of the JV or Leader with bidder's details mentioned (Cl. 24.iii.B(d)).
Step I · TBA Column: "Deviations"

Checking for Deviations

Deviations are conditions or modifications a bidder has put in their techno-commercial bid that differ from what the NIT/GCC/SCC specifies.

Why deviations must be checked in TBA

IOCL needs to know upfront if a bidder is willing to accept the full contractual terms. A deviation on a critical commercial or safety clause discovered after award creates disputes and potential losses. The TBA flags deviations so they can be resolved — or the bid rejected — before the price bid is opened.

Cl. 23.e
No deviation to GCC/SCC is permitted in this tendered job.
Cl. 24.v B(c)
Bid is liable for rejection if the bidder stipulates his own conditions and refuses to withdraw them.
How to handle deviations
  1. Review the bidder's techno-commercial bid for any conditions, caveats, or modifications to GCC/SCC terms.
  2. If deviation found: record it specifically in the Deviations column (e.g., "Bidder has sought deviation on defect liability period").
  3. Since no deviation is permitted (Cl. 23.e), raise a query asking the bidder to unconditionally withdraw the deviation.
  4. If the bidder refuses to withdraw in query reply: recommend rejection citing Cl. 24.v.B(c).
  5. If no deviations: write "NIL" — never leave blank, as blank implies the column was not checked.
Step J · TBA Column: "Whether meeting Addl. Tech. PQC"

Additional Technical PQC Check

Some tenders have additional technical requirements beyond the standard ATO and SWO criteria — for example, specific certifications, equipment ownership, or key personnel qualifications.

Cl. 12
Additional Technical Requirement (PQC), if any: Not Applicable
💡 For This Tender

Since Cl. 12 says "Not Applicable," mark this column as "N/A" for all bidders. Do not leave blank — write N/A explicitly to demonstrate the column was reviewed, not skipped. In future tenders where Addl. PQC exists (e.g., specific tool/equipment ownership, key personnel CVs, ISO certifications), this column becomes a pass/fail gate.

Why this column exists even when N/A

The TBA format is a universal document used across all IOCL tenders. By always having this column, evaluators are forced to actively check the NIT for any additional requirements rather than assuming there are none. The N/A entry is an affirmative confirmation, not an omission.

Step K · TBA Column: "Remarks"

Remarks — The Most Important Column to Write Well

The Remarks column is where the evaluator's professional judgment is documented. A vague remark is almost as bad as no remark. Here's how to write remarks that stand up to scrutiny.

Why Remarks must be specific and clause-referenced

The TBA is a legal document. If a rejected bidder challenges the decision — through court, arbitration, or CVC inquiry — the Remarks column is the evaluator's primary defence. Generic remarks like "does not meet PQC" are insufficient. Specific remarks like "Executed value of ₹302.45 Lacs (from CC dated 15.03.2022) is below the required ₹328.36 Lacs for 3-work option as per NIT Cl. 11.2" are defensible.

How to write good Remarks
  1. For Accepted bidders: Note the qualifying WO(s) and the specific PQC option met (e.g., "Qualifies under 2-job option: WO#1 executed value ₹458.20L and WO#2 executed value ₹441.00L, both ≥ ₹437.81L as per Cl. 11.2 Option 2").
  2. For Queries: State specifically what is missing or unclear (e.g., "CC for WO#2 does not mention GST component. Bidder asked to submit revised CC or separate certificate per Cl. 24.iii.B(k)").
  3. For Rejected bidders: State the specific deficiency with values and clause reference (e.g., "Highest executed value of submitted similar WO is ₹510.00L, below ₹547.26L required for 1-job option, and only 1 similar WO submitted — Option B and C not applicable. Offer rejected per Cl. 24.v.A(a)").
Cl. 24.vii
Offers with incomplete/irrelevant documents or anomalies are liable for rejection without any communication. Hence bidders are advised to take utmost care while uploading prequalification documents.
💡 Query vs. Rejection

Not every deficiency warrants immediate rejection. On Initial TBA, raise a query for: missing documents that could be submitted, unclear values that could be clarified, or ambiguous scope descriptions. Reserve outright rejection for: clear non-fulfillment of PQC values, forged/misrepresented documents, or refusal to withdraw unacceptable conditions.

Step L · TBA Column: "Final Technical Recommendation"

The Final Recommendation

The last column crystallises all preceding evaluation into a single decision: Accepted, Rejected, or Query. This drives everything that happens next.

DecisionWhen to Use & What Happens Next
ACCEPTED
Bidder meets all PQC criteria: ATO, SWO (under one of the 3 options), similar nature confirmed, CC with executed value verified, no outstanding deviations, all mandatory documents submitted. Price bid of this bidder will be opened.
REJECTED
Bidder fails to meet one or more mandatory PQC criteria and deficiency is not rectifiable by query/clarification. Price bid will NOT be opened. Must document reason in Remarks column with clause reference.
QUERY
Bidder's documents are incomplete or ambiguous — but the deficiency could potentially be resolved by seeking clarification. Issue the query, re-evaluate on Final TBA after replies. Move to Accepted or Rejected on Final TBA.
Cl. 22
Cl. 22
Job Nature: Critical. No relaxation on PQC is allowed to Startup-India registered bidders and MSE bidders. The evaluator cannot use discretion to relax PQC for any bidder in a Critical job.
⚠️ Signature & Approval

The completed TBA must be signed by the evaluator (Prepared By), reviewed by a senior officer (Checked By), and approved by DGM (Contracts) as per IWPM Clause 4.3.1 + R&R Cl. 20(viii). An unsigned TBA has no formal validity and cannot be used as basis for bid opening or rejection communication.
